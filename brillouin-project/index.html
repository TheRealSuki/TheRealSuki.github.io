Brillouin Scattering Experiment

Progress Update: June 11th - July 18th

Characterizing the Spontaneous Brillouin Regime

Project Overview & Timeline

1st Update: Foundational Understanding

  • Developed a comprehensive understanding of three key research papers.
  • Focused on core concepts: the $g^{(2)}(\tau)$ function and Brillouin scattering.
  • Established the relevance of OSA, heterodyne, and photon counting techniques for the project goals.

2nd Update: Initial Lab Work & OSA

  • Commenced hands-on lab sessions, repeating and verifying initial setups.
  • Performed OSA measurements, collecting data both manually and via computer, with analysis uploaded to GitHub.
  • Investigated the effects of polarisation, confirming the need for a polariser/filter for the single-mode (SM) cable and observing sensitivity to polarisation direction.

3rd Update: Heterodyne Measurements

  • Analyzed OSA data, confirming expected GHz-range phenomena but highlighting the need for MHz-region resolution.
  • Implemented the heterodyne measurement setup and achieved good Lorentzian fits (r > 0.95) with a linewidth of ~30MHz, consistent with literature.
  • Identified an unexpected electronic peak (~368MHz offset) requiring further investigation.
  • Next step: Deeper comparison with theoretical results from Boyd and preparing for $g^{(2)}(\tau)$ measurements.

4th Update: Photon Counting & Filter Testing

  • Began setup for photon counting. Initial measurements without filters showed coherent Rayleigh light as expected.
  • Systematically tested frequency filters (AOS 10GHz, 30GHz; Cavity 100MHz).
  • Successfully observed thermal light (a central peak in the correlation function) using the 100MHz cavity filter, which was enhanced by adding a 10GHz AOS.

5th Update: Advanced Filtering & Strategy

  • Gained extensive experience with a wider range of filters (Cavity, 1GHz, 4GHz, 10GHz, 30GHz AOS).
  • Determined that in the spontaneous regime, cavity filters are essential for achieving the required SNR to observe thermal light and beating patterns. Non-cavity filters required entering the stimulated (lasing) regime.
  • Defined two strategic paths forward: 1) Characterize the slightly lasing regime with 1GHz/4GHz filters, and 2) Use a cavity filter to isolate the first three acoustic modes in the spontaneous regime.
  • Planned the final experimental stage: simultaneous heterodyne and photon counting measurements.

6th Update (Week ending 18th July): Final Setup & Data Acquisition

  • Constructed the final experimental setup for simultaneous heterodyne and photon counting.
  • Conducted long-duration measurements with various filter combinations (1GHz, Cavity+1GHz, etc.), confirming that the cavity filter is necessary to observe beating in the spontaneous regime.
  • Acquired corresponding heterodyne and photon statistics data, crucial for linking the two measurement types theoretically.
  • Next step: Begin calculations to connect heterodyne spectra with $g^{(2)}(\tau)$ results.

Stage 1: OSA Measurements

Initial Setup & OSA Verification (June 11th)

  • Experimental setup established and verified using an Optical Spectrum Analyzer (OSA).
  • Laser power measurements and initial lasing threshold checks were completed.
  • Figured out how to save OSA data for measurements.
  • Polarizers and waveplates were used to manage power; confirmed that for SM fibre, polarization changes don't affect OSA output significantly.

Results

  • Clear observation of Stokes and Anti-Stokes peaks on either side of the main Rayleigh peak.
  • Power levels were confirmed to be in the spontaneous regime, well below the lasing threshold.
  • Data successfully exported in a usable format for offline analysis and record-keeping.

Stage 2: Heterodyne Measurements

Heterodyne Setup & Analysis (June 18th - 23rd)

  • Switched to heterodyne measurements for better resolution.
  • Successfully lined up the Local Oscillator (LO) peak with the Brillouin peak to measure Stokes, Anti-Stokes, and Rayleigh components.
  • An "extra peak" was observed, identified as a phantom electronic artifact, which was decided to be ignored for fitting.

Results

  • High-resolution spectrum reveals complex mode competition within the Stokes peak, especially near the lasing threshold.
  • The phantom electronic peak was successfully eliminated in later measurements by optimizing the setup.
  • Distinct mode structures are visible when slightly lasing, which coalesce into a single dominant mode well above the threshold.

Stage 3: Photon Counting

Initial Photon Counting Setup & Filter Tests (June 25th - July 13th)

  • Initiated the $g^{(2)}(\tau)$ experiment for photon statistics measurement.
  • Tested various frequency filters, including a cavity filter, to isolate the thermal Brillouin signal from the dominant coherent Rayleigh signal.

Results

  • The cavity filter proved most effective, showing a clear thermal bunching peak with $g^{(2)}(0) \approx 1.92$.
  • Combining filters (Cavity + 1 GHz) and performing longer measurements improved signal quality, revealing finer details in the correlation function.
  • Different bin widths highlight different features: 1ns shows the overall peak shape, while 0.1ns reveals fast beating dynamics.

Stage 4: Integrated Setup

Integrated Measurements & Ongoing Filter Challenges (July 14th - 18th)

  • Experiments continued with combined heterodyne and photon counting setups.
  • Identified low Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) in the spontaneous regime as a significant challenge, especially for non-cavity filters.
  • Latest cavity results showed good thermal light in the spontaneous regime with visible beating, confirming the viability of the technique.
  • For non-cavity filters, the spontaneous regime has poor SNR, meaning no bunching or beating is visible, only noise.

Results

  • The cavity filter remains the most effective method for isolating spontaneous Brillouin scattering for photon statistics measurements at low power.
  • Photon counting with the 1 GHz filter only shows a thermal peak when driven into the stimulated (lasing) regime due to SNR issues.
  • Longer integration times are critical for resolving the thermal peak, especially with narrower filters where the signal is weaker.

Current Status & Next Steps

  • Successfully observed thermal light features and beating patterns using the cavity filter in the spontaneous regime.
  • The 1 GHz filter's performance is currently limited by SNR in the spontaneous regime.
